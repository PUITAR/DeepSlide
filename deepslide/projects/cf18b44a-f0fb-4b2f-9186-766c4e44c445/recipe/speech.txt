The mixture of experts architecture provides efficient parameter-activation scaling. Instead of using all parameters for every token, only relevant experts are activated per token, significantly reducing computational cost while maintaining the full model capacity. This enables deploying larger model sizes with manageable inference costs. The architecture supports diverse task capabilities through specialized experts that handle different types of inputs or domains. Additionally, it provides efficient memory usage during inference, making it practical for real-world deployment.

Qwen3.5 introduces several key improvements over previous models. First, it enhances multimodal agentic performance through early-fusion training on large multimodal corpora, which better aligns visual understanding with language reasoning. This results in improved performance on vision-language benchmarks. Second, the model achieves significant computational efficiency gains through Gated Delta Networks that reduce inference overhead and sparse MoE routing with 512 experts where only 10 plus 1 shared expert are activated per token. This means the model has 397 billion total parameters but only 17 billion active during inference. Third, Qwen3.5 demonstrates scalable reinforcement learning generalization, with RL being scaled across large collections of tool-using environments with progressively more complex task distributions.

Despite its improvements, Qwen3.5 has several limitations and open questions. First, there are unclear generalization bounds. The model lacks comprehensive theoretical analysis of its generalization capabilities, and performance boundaries across diverse real-world scenarios are not well-defined. Transfer learning limits to novel domains remain uncertain. Second, scaling behavior details are insufficient. The exact scaling laws for multimodal performance are not provided, efficiency trade-offs at different scales are not quantified, and optimal parameter allocation strategies remain unclear. Third, there are architectural transparency gaps. Full architectural details of hybrid components are not disclosed, training curriculum specifics for RL scaling are limited, and multimodal fusion mechanisms are only partially described in the available documentation.

The architecture of Qwen3.5 incorporates several efficiency features. It uses a sparse mixture-of-experts design with 512 experts, but only 10 plus 1 shared expert are activated per token, providing significant parameter efficiency with 397 billion total parameters versus only 17 billion active during inference. The hybrid backbone combines Gated DeltaNet and Attention layers, and supports extended context windows of 262 thousand tokens natively, extensible to over 1 million tokens. However, these efficiency gains come with trade-offs. While sparse activation reduces computational costs, it introduces complexity in expert routing and load balancing, and there's memory overhead for storing all expert parameters even though only a subset are used during inference.
